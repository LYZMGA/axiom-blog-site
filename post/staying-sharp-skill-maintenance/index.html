Staying Sharp — How I Keep Security Skills From Going Stale

2026-06-17 · 1 min read · Notes · career learning ctf habits

Personal notes on maintaining breadth across red, blue, and cloud without burning out: the daily reading habit that actually sticks, a lab rotation schedule, CTF cadence, and an honest list of what's worth paying for versus what isn't.

The hardest part of security isn't learning a skill — it's keeping three or four unrelated skill sets simultaneously current while the field underneath all of them keeps moving. This is the actual schedule I run, not an aspirational one.

The Daily Reading Habit That Actually Sticks

Anything requiring more than 15 minutes a day dies within two weeks — that's just true for me, and probably for most people with a full-time job. What survives is a tight, fixed rotation, read in the same order every morning before anything else gets a chance to eat the time slot:

1. One vendor/CERT advisory feed (CISA KEV additions, or a vendor security advisory RSS) — five minutes, just enough to know what's actively being exploited this week.

2. One technical writeup, rotating weekly between a red-team source (a fresh CTF writeup, a new tool release note) and a blue-team source (a detection engineering post, a DFIR case study). Alternating the angle deliberately prevents the read-only-what-I-already-do trap.

3. Skim, don't deep-read, everything else. Headlines and abstracts for a broader news aggregator are enough to flag what deserves a real read later — most days, nothing does, and that's fine.

The habit is the schedule, not the content

What I read each day matters less than reading at a fixed time every day. The habit that survives is the one tied to an existing routine (coffee, commute, first 15 minutes at the desk) — not the one that depends on remembering to do it.

Lab Rotation Schedule

Breadth across red, blue, and cloud doesn't happen by accident — without a forced rotation, time naturally drifts toward whichever skill is most fun right now, and the other two atrophy quietly until a job interview or an engagement exposes the gap. The rotation I run, in four-week blocks:

WeekFocusConcrete output
1Offensive — HTB/CTF box or a new exploitation techniqueOne rooted box or one working PoC for a technique I hadn't built before
2Defensive — detection engineering against my home labOne new Sigma rule, validated by re-running the attack from week 1 against it
3Cloud — IAM misconfig hunting in a disposable AWS/Azure sandboxOne documented privilege-escalation path found and remediated
4Consolidation — write up whatever from weeks 1–3 was actually novelOne blog post, or at minimum, one set of personal notes worth re-reading later

Week 2 deliberately reuses week 1's output — testing a detection against the exact attack you just ran yourself is far more honest feedback than testing it against a canned dataset. This is the single highest-value link in the whole rotation.

CTF Cadence

I don't play every weekend — that's a fast route to burnout and diminishing returns once you've already internalised the recurring patterns (see the companion notes on that). What I actually do: one CTF a month, picked deliberately for a category I'm weakest in, not the category I'd enjoy most. If pwn is rusty, that month's CTF is pwn-heavy, even if web would be more fun. The goal of a monthly CTF isn't points on a leaderboard — it's a forcing function against skill rot in the area I'd otherwise avoid.

Paid vs. Free — An Honest List

ResourceVerdictWhy
HTB / TryHackMe subscriptionWorth paying forRetired-box writeups and active machines are worth more than the subscription cost in structured practice time alone
A home lab's cloud compute (sandbox AWS/Azure accounts)Worth paying for, in small amountsA real misconfigured IAM policy in a real cloud console teaches more in an hour than any slide deck — keep spend capped with billing alerts
Most certification bootcamp coursesSkip, build the lab insteadThe certification itself can be worth it for a resume line; the bootcamp video content is usually a slower version of free documentation
Vendor threat intel platform subscriptions (personal use)SkipCISA KEV, vendor blogs, and free Twitter/RSS feeds from researchers cover the same ground for an individual's needs — paid TI platforms earn their cost at organisational scale, not for one person's reading habit
A dedicated, properly resourced home labWorth paying forThe single highest-leverage spend on this list — see the companion notes on lab mistakes for why under-resourcing it defeats the purpose
The actual filter

Before paying for anything, the question I ask is: does this let me do something hands-on that I otherwise couldn't, or does it just deliver the same information in a more polished format? Hands-on access (lab compute, vulnerable targets, a real cloud sandbox) consistently clears that bar. Content consumption, almost as consistently, doesn't — there's already more free, high-quality technical writing than anyone can read.
