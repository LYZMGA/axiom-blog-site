Physical Security Testing — From Badge Clone to Domain Admin

2026-06-17 · 2 min read · Security · physical-security red-team social-engineering rfid rogue-device

A red team physical assessment methodology, start to finish: the pretexts that get a door opened before you touch a lock, badge cloning with a Proxmark3, mechanical bypass, and turning thirty seconds of physical access into a persistent foothold via a dropped implant.

Authorization first, always

Every technique below requires a signed statement of work with explicit physical-testing authorization, a get-out-of-jail letter carried on your person during the engagement, and a pre-agreed emergency contact who can vouch for the test to building security or law enforcement in real time. Physical engagements are the category most likely to result in an actual police response if this groundwork is skipped.

Phase 1 — Pretexts That Open Doors

The most reliable way through a physical control is not defeating it — it's getting someone with legitimate access to defeat it for you. Effective pretexts share three properties: they give the target a reason to want to help rather than just permission to, they match something plausible for that specific facility, and they create urgency without raising suspicion.

PretextWhy it worksBest against
Delivery driver, arms full of boxesHolding a door for someone visibly burdened is an automatic social reflex; nobody wants to be the person who didn't helpLoading docks, side entrances, office buildings with regular courier traffic
New contractor / vendor technicianPlausible deniability for not having a badge yet; staff don't want to be the one who blocks a scheduled vendor visitFacilities with frequent third-party maintenance (HVAC, telecom, cleaning contractors)
Forgot-my-badge employee, tailgatingMost people will hold a door for someone who looks like they belong and is mid-conversation on a phoneTailgating through any badge-controlled door with foot traffic
Fire/safety inspectorAuthority-figure pretext; people rarely challenge someone who appears to be performing a compliance functionFacilities with visible fire-safety signage or known recent inspection cycles
Wardrobe and props beat technique

A high-visibility vest, a clipboard, and a lanyard with literally any badge-shaped object on it does more to defeat human scrutiny than any lockpicking skill will. Most successful physical engagements are won in the wardrobe choice made before arriving on site, not in anything done at the door.

Phase 2 — Badge Cloning with a Proxmark3

Once inside (or even from outside a card-reader's read range, for low-frequency systems), a Proxmark3 reads and clones the credential most facilities still rely on. Low-frequency (125 kHz) prox cards — HID ProxCard, EM4100 — have no cryptographic protection at all and clone in seconds. High-frequency (13.56 MHz) cards — MIFARE Classic, iCLASS — vary: MIFARE Classic's encryption (Crypto-1) has been broken for over a decade and is crackable in the field; iCLASS Standard Security is similarly broken; both DESFire EV2/EV3 and iCLASS SE/Seos use modern cryptography that resists field cloning entirely.

text Proxmark3 — LF clone (HID/EM4100, no crypto) 
[usb] pm3 --> lf search
[usb] pm3 --> lf hid read
[usb] pm3 --> lf hid clone -r <captured raw ID>
# Or write directly to a T5577 blank:
[usb] pm3 --> lf em 410x clone --id <captured ID>
text Proxmark3 — MIFARE Classic (crackable via nested/darkside attack) 
[usb] pm3 --> hf mf info
[usb] pm3 --> hf mf autopwn
# Recovers all sector keys via nested/hardnested attack, dumps full card,
# writes the dump to a Magic Card (UID-changeable clone)
[usb] pm3 --> hf mf restore -f dumped_card.bin
Read range is the real-world constraint

Field cloning a badge usually doesn't mean asking someone to hand it over — it means brushing a Proxmark3 (or a long-range reading antenna built into something innocuous, like a bag) within a few centimetres of a target's badge while they're not paying attention: in a crowded elevator, queuing for coffee, sitting next to them on public transport if the engagement scope and pretext support that level of contact. Plan the read opportunity as carefully as the technical clone itself — it's usually the harder half.

Phase 3 — Mechanical and Electronic Lock Bypass

When a door isn't badge-controlled, or as a fallback when cloning isn't viable, mechanical bypass covers the rest:

Lock picking (standard pin-tumbler locks) — a tension wrench and pick set defeats the majority of commercial-grade pin-tumber locks within minutes once the technique is practised; this is a skill that requires hands-on repetition, not just reading about it.

Bypass tools over picking, when speed matters — under-door tools to push a request-to-exit button from outside, shimming a spring-latch (non-deadbolt) door, or a "bump key" for pin-tumbler locks that tolerates more imprecision than a full pick under time pressure.

Electronic strike attacks — many electronic door strikes fail open or can be defeated by a relay attack on the wiring if the strike's control wiring is accessible from an unsecured side (a common finding on doors where the strike was retrofitted without re-running conduit properly).

Phase 4 — Deploying a Raspberry Pi Implant

Physical access that doesn't establish a persistent foothold is a one-time win. A small single-board computer dropped on an internal network during the brief window of access converts "I got into the building" into "I have ongoing remote access," which is almost always the more valuable deliverable for the client.

text Implant build checklist 
Hardware: Raspberry Pi Zero 2 W (small, low power draw, easy to hide behind/under furniture or in an unused wall jack enclosure)

Pre-configured before deployment:
  - Reverse shell / C2 beacon (e.g. a lightweight agent calling out over HTTPS
    to an attacker-controlled listener) set to auto-start on boot
  - Outbound-only connectivity assumed -- configured to beacon out, never to
    listen for inbound connections, to survive typical egress-only firewall rules
  - 4G/LTE fallback module if budget/scope allows -- survives the implant
    being placed on a network segment with port security or NAC that blocks
    an unrecognised MAC address
  - Physically disguised as innocuous infrastructure (a "spare" PoE injector,
    taped under a desk, behind a printer) -- visual plausibility matters as
    much as the network configuration

Deployment:
  - Plug into any live, unsecured network jack found during the walk (conference
    rooms and unused desks are reliable; physically verify link lights before leaving)
  - Confirm beacon check-in remotely before physically leaving the building,
    if the engagement timeline allows -- a dead implant found on day two
    isn't a finding, it's a wasted trip
Document chain of custody on every implant

Every dropped device needs a documented serial number, deployment timestamp, and physical location, reported to the client's emergency contact at engagement end regardless of whether it was recovered. An undocumented Raspberry Pi found six months later by IT, with no record it was an authorised test, is exactly the kind of incident that turns a successful engagement into a real security event — and a legal problem for you specifically.

From Implant to Domain Admin

Once the implant has a beacon, the rest of the path is identical to any internal network engagement: enumerate the segment it landed on, look for unauthenticated SMB/LDAP exposure, attempt the standard AD privilege-escalation chain (Kerberoasting, AS-REP roasting, ACL abuse, delegation attacks — covered step-by-step in the companion AD privilege escalation writeup) from that foothold through to Domain Admin. The physical phase's entire job is producing exactly this foothold; everything after it is conventional internal-network tradecraft.
